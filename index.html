About

I am a Pathology Resident and Academic Clinical Lecturer based in Edinburgh, UK. I hold a joint appointment with NHS Lothian and the University of Edinburgh, where I am conducting post-doctoral research work hosted by the Pollard lab at the Centre for Regenerative Medicine, Institute for Regeneration and Repair. I am funded through the Edinburgh Clinical Academic Training (ECAT) programme under the Scottish Clinical Research Excellence Development Scheme (SCREDS).

My research focuses on somatic and cancer evolution, with a particular interest in how genomic instability — including aneuploidy and extrachromosomal DNA (ecDNA) — drives tumour initiation, progression, and recurrence. I am especially keen on integrating spatial multi-omic approaches with histo-morphological features to better understand these processes in clinically relevant contexts.

I’m always interested in new collaborations and ideas at the intersection of genomic instability, spatial biology, and pathology. Please feel free to get in touch if you’d like to explore potential synergies.

Research

We are interested in tracing and targeting genomic instability in cancers by studying tumours predominantly in their native tissue contexts. By combining histopathological expertise with advanced genomic and spatial technologies, we aim to bridge the gap between molecular mechanisms and clinical impact. Our work focuses on three interconnected themes:

Mechanisms underlying genomic instability

We investigate how genomic instability arises, persists, and is selected for during tumour evolution. Particular emphasis is placed on extrachromosomal DNA (ecDNA), chromothripsis, and other structural variants, including their lineage-specific vulnerabilities (e.g., in neural progenitors) and interplay with DNA double-strand breaks, R-loops, and mitochondrial–nuclear crosstalk. Using a combination of genome-edited cell models, patient-derived samples, and large whole-genome sequencing datasets, we seek to uncover the rules governing ecDNA formation, amplification, and inheritance in different tumour types.

Morphology-aware spatial multi-omics

We develop and apply spatially resolved approaches that preserve and leverage tissue morphology and cellular context. By integrating histology-guided single-nucleus multi-omics (e.g., Slide-tags, scONE-seq, and spatial transcriptomics) with high-resolution imaging, we map the spatial organisation of genomic instability, ecDNA-driven heterogeneity, and microenvironmental niches within intact tumours and premalignant lesions. This “morphology-aware” framework enables precise linking of molecular features to histopathological patterns, improving biological insight and diagnostic relevance.

Biomarkers and therapeutics

We translate mechanistic and spatial insights into clinically actionable tools. This includes the development of liquid biopsy approaches, as well as the identification of synthetic-lethal vulnerabilities and novel therapeutic targets. We are particularly focused on cancers of unmet need, particularly malignant brain tumours such as glioblastoma and medulloblastoma, high-risk neuroblastoma, high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma, and others, with the ultimate goal of improving early detection, risk stratification, and precision treatment strategies.
